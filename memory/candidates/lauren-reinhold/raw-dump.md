### The Fundamental Math Problem

Kansas 1st is R+16. Tracey Mann wins with ~70%. For any challenger to win:

- A Democrat would need roughly every Democratic vote PLUS a massive crossover of Republican/independent voters
- The 2022 Kansas abortion vote showed rural Kansans can break with the GOP on specific issues, but that hasn't translated to candidate elections
- The Lawrence redistricting adds Democratic votes but also adds cultural friction with western Kansas voters
- Both Democrats are from Lawrence, which is a significant liability in a district where Lawrence is viewed with suspicion by many rural voters

# PART ONE: THE TWO BALLOT MEASURES

These are constitutional amendments. They change the Kansas Constitution itself. You vote YES or NO. There is no candidate -- just the question.

---

## AMENDMENT 1: Should Kansas Voters Elect Supreme Court Justices?

**On your ballot: August 4, 2026 (PRIMARY election -- not November)**

---

### What This Amendment Would Do

Right now, when there is an opening on the Kansas Supreme Court, a 9-person commission (5 lawyers elected by other lawyers, 4 non-lawyers picked by the governor) reviews the applicants, picks 3 finalists, and sends those names to the governor. The governor picks one. Then every 6 years, you get to vote YES or NO on keeping that justice (a "retention vote").

This amendment would scrap that entire system. Instead, you would vote for Supreme Court justices the same way you vote for governor or senator -- in regular elections. Seven justices would be phased in through elections in 2028, 2030, and 2032. The 9-person nominating commission would be abolished permanently.

**The catch nobody is talking about:** The amendment does NOT say whether these elections would be partisan (Republican vs. Democrat) or nonpartisan. It does NOT say how campaigns would be financed. It does NOT say what the nomination process would look like. ALL of those details would be decided later by the Kansas Legislature -- which is controlled by a Republican supermajority. As retired Kansas Supreme Court Justice Carol Beier put it: "It is exceedingly vague. It will be decided by the Legislature. Do you trust them?"

---

### Why This Exists -- The Real History

This fight did not come out of nowhere. There are three things that made Kansas Republicans angry enough to put this on the ballot:

**1. The Abortion Ruling (2019):** The Kansas Supreme Court ruled 6-1 that the Kansas Constitution protects a right to "personal autonomy," including the right to decide whether to continue a pregnancy. This made Kansas one of the strongest states for abortion rights -- decided by appointed justices, not elected ones.

**2. The School Funding Wars (2005-2024):** For nearly 20 years, the Kansas Supreme Court told the Legislature it was not funding public schools adequately. The court repeatedly ordered lawmakers to spend more money on education. Conservative legislators were furious about being told how to spend taxpayer money by judges they did not elect.

**3. Kobach's Losses in Court:** Attorney General Kris Kobach has had multiple laws struck down by Kansas courts, including his proof-of-citizenship voter registration law.

Kansas Reflector explicitly headlined their coverage: "Kansas Senate adopts plan to elect Supreme Court justices, a step toward overturning abortion rights."

**The 1958 origin story:** Kansas adopted its current system after one of the most embarrassing scandals in state history. In 1956-57, Governor Fred Hall lost his reelection bid, his friend the Chief Justice conveniently resigned, Hall himself resigned with 11 days left in office, and his Lieutenant Governor (who had to be pulled out of a hospital by a Highway Patrolman) was briefly elevated to Governor just long enough to appoint Hall to the Supreme Court. Kansans were so disgusted by this backroom deal that they voted to create the nominating commission system in 1958. It has been in place for 67 years.

---

### Why Supporters Want This

**Their core argument:** The people should choose their judges. Period. Democracy means voting.

Attorney General Kris Kobach: "People, not only lawyers in smoke-filled back rooms, should have a voice in selecting Kansas Supreme Court justices."

Americans for Prosperity-Kansas (Koch-funded conservative group): "The current process involves a commission of trial attorneys selecting nominees behind closed doors with no input from Kansans or their elected legislators."

Kobach has cited polling showing 74% of Kansans support directly electing Supreme Court justices. **However:** That polling came from Kobach's own office, and the way you ask the question matters enormously. "Should you get to vote for judges?" always polls well. "Should judges have to raise millions from special interests to win partisan campaigns?" polls very differently. No independent public polling on this specific amendment has been released.

**Who voted for it in the Legislature:** The amendment passed the Kansas Senate 27-13 and the House 84-40. It required a two-thirds supermajority in both chambers. 93% of Republicans voted YES. Democrats almost unanimously voted NO. It passed by the absolute minimum number of votes -- if ONE more legislator in either chamber had voted no, it would not be on your ballot.

---

### Why Opponents Fight This

**Their core argument:** This will turn Kansas courts into political war zones funded by out-of-state billionaires.

Micah Kubic, ACLU of Kansas: "Partisan elections quickly become fights between out-of-state, big money interests. These elections become less about the voters and constituents of the state and more an opportunity for out-of-state big money interests to wage war against one another."

Retired Justice Carol Beier: "Opening the process up to expensive popular elections financed by special interests was contrary to the idea of an independent judiciary."

Teresa Woody, Kansas Appleseed: "It would be impossible for elections of Supreme Court justices to be anything other than partisan brawls."

Rep. Rui Xu (D-Westwood): "Republicans are mad that our Supreme Court wanted us to fully fund school and not ban abortions so now they want to change the Supreme Court."

**Organizations opposing:** Kansas Bar Association, ACLU of Kansas, American Federation of Teachers-Kansas, Kansas Appleseed Center for Law and Justice, Kansas Association of School Boards, Kansas Women Attorneys Association, Planned Parenthood Great Plains Votes, VoteNoKansas.org, Stand Up Blue Valley.

---

### What You Need to Know -- The Facts That Cut Through the Spin

**FACT 1: This is on the August PRIMARY ballot, not November.** Primary turnout in Kansas is typically 20-25%, compared to 50-60% in November. Republican voters dominate primary turnout because they have more contested races. Putting it on the primary ballot is widely seen as a strategy to pass it with a smaller, more conservative electorate. The exact same strategy was used for the 2022 abortion amendment -- and it backfired spectacularly when turnout surged to nearly 50% and the amendment was crushed 59-41.

**FACT 2: The amendment is deliberately vague.** It says justices will be elected but leaves every important detail -- partisan vs. nonpartisan, campaign finance rules, nomination process -- to the Legislature. The Legislature has a Republican supermajority.

**FACT 3: Other states that elect judges have seen massive spending.** Wisconsin's 2025 Supreme Court race cost $144.5 million for a single seat. Elon Musk alone spent over $25 million on one side. North Carolina elected a conservative court majority, and those new justices immediately reheard and reversed decisions on redistricting and voting rights that had been issued just months earlier.

**FACT 4: The opposition has raised nearly $2 million.** The pro-side has dark money backing (see below).

**FACT 5: Kansas is the ONLY state that uses a bar-controlled commission** where lawyers elected by other lawyers hold the majority. Supporters call this elitist. Opponents call it a feature, not a bug -- it keeps judges qualified and independent.

---

### Who Is Behind It -- Follow the Money

**Pro-Amendment (YES):**
- **Kansans for Democracy** -- a 501(c)(4) nonprofit that is NOT required to disclose its donors -- booked approximately $802,000 in television ads running from July 7 through August 4. The Sunflower State Journal reports this group has "a conservative connection." This is textbook dark money: nearly $1 million in ads and the public cannot find out who is paying for them.
- **Americans for Prosperity-Kansas** (funded by the Koch network)
- **Kansas Chamber United for Business**
- **Kansas Policy Institute** (conservative think tank)

**Anti-Amendment (NO):**
- The opposition campaign (VoteNoKansas.org and allies) has raised nearly $2 million according to Sunflower State Journal reporting.
- Kansas Bar Association, ACLU, teacher unions, school board associations, and others listed above.

---

### What Happened Elsewhere -- Real Examples

**Wisconsin (2025):** One Supreme Court seat. $144.5 million spent. Elon Musk spent $25 million+ on one candidate. Illinois Governor JB Pritzker and George Soros funded the other side. It became a national proxy war over abortion, Trump, and redistricting -- not about Kansas-style local concerns. The losing candidate's supporters included $4.3 million from Wisconsin Manufacturers and Commerce, $4 million from Fair Courts America (tied to conservative megadonor Richard Uihlein), and $3.2 million from Americans for Prosperity (Koch). This is what Kansas could look like.

**North Carolina (2022):** After partisan elections flipped the court's majority to conservative, the new justices took the extraordinary step of rehearing and reversing several predecessor decisions on redistricting and voting rights -- decisions issued just months earlier. Opponents say this is exactly what would happen in Kansas with abortion rights.

**Pennsylvania:** Supreme Court races have become multi-million-dollar partisan battles with spending increasing every cycle.

**The academic evidence:** Research in the Journal of Public Economics found that judges selected through nonpartisan processes produce higher-quality work than judges chosen in partisan elections. The Brennan Center for Justice documented that campaign spending and special interest influence in state supreme court elections has increased dramatically nationwide.

## AMENDMENT 2: Should the Kansas Constitution Say "Only Citizens" Can Vote?

**On your ballot: November 3, 2026 (GENERAL election)**

---

### What This Amendment Would Do

Right now, the Kansas Constitution says: "Every citizen of the United States who has attained the age of eighteen years and who resides in the voting area in which he or she seeks to vote shall be deemed a qualified elector."

This amendment would change it to: "No person shall be deemed a qualified elector unless such person: (1) Is a citizen of the United States..."

In plain English: The current Constitution says "every citizen CAN vote." The amendment would change it to "ONLY citizens can vote." Same idea, different framing -- from welcoming language to restricting language.

---

### What This Actually Changes Legally

**Honest answer: Almost nothing. Right now.**

It is ALREADY illegal for non-citizens to vote in Kansas. It has been illegal under both Kansas state law and federal law for as long as Kansas has been a state. This amendment does not create any new rule. It restates what is already the law using stronger, exclusionary language.

The ballot question itself says as much: "This amendment would clarify that only a person who is a citizen of the United States is eligible to vote in this state."

---

### Is Non-Citizen Voting Actually a Problem in Kansas?

**No. The evidence is overwhelming.**

When Kris Kobach was Secretary of State, he championed the Kansas SAFE Act (2011), which required documentary proof of citizenship (birth certificate or passport) to register to vote. The ACLU sued. The case went to federal court. Here is what the court found:

- Over nearly 20 years, Kansas officials confirmed only **39 non-citizens** on the voter rolls (many due to administrative error at the DMV, not intentional fraud)
- Meanwhile, Kobach's law **blocked 31,089 eligible American citizens** from registering to vote
- That is a ratio of roughly 1 non-citizen for every 797 blocked citizens

Federal Judge Julie Robinson's conclusion: **"There is no iceberg; only an icicle, largely created by confusion and administrative error."**

The law was struck down as unconstitutional. The 10th Circuit Court of Appeals upheld the ruling. The U.S. Supreme Court declined to hear the case.

---

### Why Supporters Want This

Their argument is straightforward: Voting should be exclusively for citizens, and the Constitution should say so clearly and forcefully. They point out that similar amendments passed in all 8 states where they appeared on the 2024 ballot, often with 65-80% of the vote. It is a matter of principle, even if non-citizen voting is not currently a problem.

The amendment passed the Kansas House 90-28 and the Senate 37-3. Unlike the judicial amendment, this had meaningful bipartisan support -- 9 House Democrats voted YES.

---

### Why Opponents Fight This

**ACLU of Kansas:** "The language in HCR 5004 could be weaponized to justify a return to restrictive voter registration laws, as it explicitly conditions voter eligibility on U.S. citizenship, despite no evidence of noncitizen voting in Kansas elections."

**Loud Light Civic Action:** "This amendment does nothing to strengthen our Constitution, it appears to do nothing at all legally, yet it will cause confusion, inflame tensions, and distract from important conversations."

The concern is not about what the amendment does today. It is about what it enables tomorrow. Specifically:

1. **Proof-of-citizenship revival:** The exclusionary language could give Kansas constitutional ammunition to re-enact a proof-of-citizenship requirement for voter registration -- the same kind of law that blocked 31,000+ citizens last time.
2. **Additional ID barriers:** Could justify requiring additional documentation beyond what is currently needed to register.
3. **Chilling effect:** The framing could discourage naturalized citizens from voting out of confusion or fear.

---

### What You Need to Know

**FACT 1: Non-citizen voting is already illegal.** This amendment does not make anything new illegal.

**FACT 2: The evidence of non-citizen voting in Kansas is nearly nonexistent.** Federal courts examined 20 years of data and found 39 cases, mostly from administrative error.

**FACT 3: Every state that has voted on this has passed it.** From 2018-2025, voters approved all 15 similar ballot measures nationwide. This will almost certainly pass in Kansas.

**FACT 4: The real debate is about the future.** Supporters see it as locking in a principle. Opponents see it as laying groundwork for restrictive voter registration laws.

**FACT 5: The Kobach connection.** As Attorney General, Kobach still pursues voter fraud cases. In November 2025, Kobach and Secretary of State Schwab announced charges in a voter fraud case. The question is whether this amendment becomes a tool for future proof-of-citizenship legislation.

## Kansas Governor
**Primary: August 4, 2026 | General: November 3, 2026**

### What Does the Governor Actually Do?

The governor is the most powerful person in Kansas state government. They sign or veto every bill the Legislature passes. They appoint the heads of nearly every state department and agency. They submit the state budget. They command the Kansas National Guard. In emergencies, they have near-absolute power over state resources. They serve a 4-year term and can serve a maximum of two consecutive terms.

### Why Does This Open Seat Matter?

Governor Laura Kelly (Democrat) is term-limited and cannot run again. This is the first open governor's race in Kansas since 2018. Open seats are rare and always attract crowded fields because there is no incumbent advantage. The winner will shape Kansas policy on taxes, education funding, Medicaid expansion, abortion access, and how the state responds to federal government changes for the next 4 years.

Kansas has a history of electing moderate governors even though the Legislature is heavily Republican. Kelly won twice as a Democrat. Before her, Republican Sam Brownback's aggressive tax cuts nearly bankrupted the state. The question for 2026 is whether Kansas voters pick a governor who will check the Legislature's power or one who will rubber-stamp it.

**Republican primary (crowded):** Former Governor Jeff Colyer (plastic surgeon, served 2018-19), Secretary of State Scott Schwab, Senate President Ty Masterson, Insurance Commissioner Vicki Schmidt, Charlotte O'Hara (former Johnson County commissioner), Stacy Rogers (Wichita businesswoman), and others.

**Democratic primary:** Senator Ethan Corson (Fairway attorney, emphasizes bipartisan deal-making, helped eliminate the grocery sales tax), Senator Cindy Holscher (Overland Park, farm-raised, led repeal of Brownback tax cuts, founded Bipartisan Women's Caucus).

## U.S. Senate: Roger Marshall vs. Challengers
**Primary: August 4, 2026 | General: November 3, 2026**

### What Does Marshall Actually Do for Kansas?

Senator Roger Marshall is a physician from Great Bend who has represented Kansas in the U.S. Senate since 2021 (before that, he was the KS-01 congressman). In the Senate, he sits on committees dealing with agriculture, health, and the budget. Kansas has not elected a Democrat to the U.S. Senate since 1932. That is not a typo. Ninety-four years. So why are 8 Democrats running?

Two reasons: First, the 2022 abortion vote proved that Kansas voters will cross party lines on the right issue. If DOGE cuts hit Kansas hard -- closing rural VA clinics, cutting farm subsidies, eliminating Social Security Administration offices in small towns -- there could be an opening. Second, Marshall's close alignment with Trump and Musk is a bet that could pay off or backfire depending on how Kansans feel about federal government cuts by November.

**Notable Democratic challengers:**

## U.S. House KS-01 (Big First): Tracey Mann
**Primary: August 4, 2026 | General: November 3, 2026**

### Why Is This Seat So Safe for Republicans?

Kansas's 1st Congressional District covers the entire western two-thirds of the state -- from the Nebraska border to the Oklahoma border, from the Colorado line almost to Topeka. It is the second-largest congressional district east of the Mississippi. It is overwhelmingly rural, agricultural, and Republican. Mann won reelection in 2024 with 69% of the vote. Mann serves on the House Agriculture Committee and chairs the Subcommittee on Livestock, Dairy, and Poultry. For western Kansas, this is the most important committee assignment possible. He has spent his time fighting for Farm Bill provisions: strengthening crop insurance, raising reference prices for ARC and PLC programs (which protect farmers when commodity prices drop), increasing funding for agricultural export programs (MAP and FMD), and pushing to set the estate tax exemption at $30 million per couple so family farms can pass to the next generation without being sold to pay taxes. He was named a "Friend of Farm Bureau" by the American Farm Bureau Federation.

In plain English: Mann's job is keeping western Kansas agriculture funded and protected at the federal level.

## Kansas Attorney General: Kobach vs. Mann (Rematch)
**Primary: August 4, 2026 | General: November 3, 2026**

### What Does the Attorney General Actually Do?

The AG is Kansas's top law enforcement officer. They prosecute major crimes that cross county lines, defend the state in lawsuits, issue legal opinions that guide state agencies, enforce consumer protection laws, go after fraud, and represent Kansas in cases at the U.S. Supreme Court. The AG's office shapes how aggressively Kansas enforces everything from environmental laws to election rules.

### Why Was the 2022 Race So Close?

Kris Kobach (Republican) beat Chris Mann (Democrat) by only 15,000 votes out of 1 million cast -- a 1.6% margin, the closest statewide race that year. Kobach is one of the most polarizing figures in Kansas politics. He is a lightning rod on immigration, voting laws, and abortion. Some voters see him as a principled conservative warrior; others see him as someone who picks high-profile fights and loses in court.

This is a direct rematch. Mann is a Lawrence attorney and former police officer turned prosecutor. He focuses on "basics of law enforcement" -- DUI prevention, victims' rights, public safety. His pitch: the AG's office should protect Kansans, not chase national headlines.

Kobach's campaign says Kansas crime has dropped to a 20-year low under his leadership. Mann says the AG should focus on the basics and "defend the rule of law."

**Why this matters:** The AG interprets and enforces Kansas laws on abortion, voting, immigration, and government transparency. Whoever holds this office shapes which laws get enforced aggressively and which ones gather dust.

## Kansas Secretary of State
**Primary: August 4, 2026 | General: November 3, 2026**

### Why Is This the Most Consequential Race Nobody Is Watching?

The Secretary of State runs Kansas elections. They decide how voter registration works, what IDs are required, how ballots are counted, whether mail-in voting is easy or hard, and how election complaints are handled. In a time when election integrity is a national flashpoint, this office has enormous power over how -- and whether -- Kansans vote.

Incumbent Scott Schwab (Republican) is leaving to run for governor.

Pat Proctor is a Republican state representative from Leavenworth who chairs the House Elections Committee. He is a retired Army officer with a doctorate in history. Here is what matters about his record:

Proctor has been one of the Legislature's strongest advocates for election law changes inspired by President Trump's claims about the 2020 election. As Elections Committee chair, he has championed bills to:
- **Repeal the 3-day grace period** for mailed ballots to arrive after Election Day
- **Ban ranked-choice voting** in Kansas
- **Ban foreign nationals** from contributing to constitutional amendment campaigns
- **Limit the Kansas Governmental Ethics Commission's** power to investigate campaign finance violations

If elected Secretary of State, he would go from writing the election rules to enforcing them.

**Other candidates:**
- **Ken Rahjes** (Republican) -- state representative from the 110th district
- **Jennifer Day** (Democrat) -- former state representative
- **Sam Lane** (Democrat)

**Why this matters for Hays:** The Secretary of State decides how easy or hard it is for every person in Ellis County to register, vote by mail, and have their vote counted. This office touches every single election on your ballot.

## Kansas Insurance Commissioner

The Insurance Commissioner regulates every insurance company doing business in Kansas -- health insurance, car insurance, homeowners insurance, crop insurance, all of it. In rural Kansas, where hospital closures and limited healthcare options are a growing crisis, the Insurance Commissioner's decisions on what health plans are available and what they can charge directly affect your family's bottom line.

## State Board of Education District 5
**Filing deadline: June 1, 2026 | Primary: August 4, 2026 | General: November 3, 2026**

### What Does the State Board of Education Actually Control?

The Kansas State Board of Education is one of the most powerful and least understood bodies in state government. It has 10 elected members (one from each district) and has constitutional authority over:

- **Curriculum standards** -- what Kansas kids learn in math, science, reading, writing, and social studies (yes, including what gets taught about evolution, history, and sex education)
- **School accreditation** -- whether schools meet state standards
- **Teacher licensing** -- who is qualified to teach in Kansas
- **Special education** -- programs for disabled, homebound, and exceptional students
- **Career and technical education** -- approval of all vocational programs
- **School nutrition** -- food service and child care food programs
- **School district organization** -- how districts are structured
- **Budget requests** -- the Board requested over $105 million in enhancements for FY2026

District 5 covers western Kansas, including Hays. The current member is **Cathy Hopkins** (Republican), elected in 2022. Her seat is up in 2026.

**Why this matters:** The Board of Education has been a battleground over science standards (evolution vs. intelligent design), sex education, school library books, and transgender student policies. In recent years, conservative and moderate factions have fought for control. The 2026 elections could shift the balance of power on the board.

**As of March 31, 2026, no candidates have filed for District 5.** The filing deadline is June 1. Watch this space -- candidates often file at the last minute.
